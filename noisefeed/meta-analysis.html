Joint Analysis

Generalized mix-effect model - league:team as random factor 
match_injury_joint_model <- match_injury |> 
  mutate(
    season_z = (season - mean(season)) / sd(season),
    match_played_z = (n_match - mean(n_match)) / sd(n_match)
  ) |> 
  lme4::glmer(
    n_injury ~ match_played_z + season_z + league + (1 | league:team),
    data = _,
    family = poisson(link = "log"),
    offset = log(match_exposure)
  )

1. Data and outcome

The dataset contains:

  • Leagues: Bundesliga, La Liga, Ligue 1, Premier League, Serie A
  • Units: Team–season observations (1,560 rows, 186 teams)
  • Outcome:
    • n_injury = number of match injuries per team per season
    • Exposure: match_exposure (match minutes or match-hours), used as an offset
  • Key predictors:
    • n_match (standardized to match_played_z): number of matches played
    • season (standardized to season_z): time trend (2010–2024)
    • league: categorical factor
  • Model family: Poisson with log link, i.e. modeling injury counts with log(injury rate) as the linear predictor.

In plain language: you’re modeling injury rates per unit of match exposure, adjusting for how many matches a team plays and how late in the time period the season is, with teams nested in leagues.

2. Joint generalized linear mixed model

2.1 Model specification

Joint model:

\[ \log(\lambda_{ij}) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \text{match\_played\_z}_{ij} + \beta_2 \text{season\_z}_{ij} + \beta_{\text{league}} + u_{\text{league:team}} , \]

with

  • \(\lambda_{ij}\) = expected injury rate for team \(j\) in league \(i\) per unit exposure
  • Offset: \(\log(\text{match\_exposure})\)
  • Random intercept: \(u_{\text{league:team}} \sim N(0, \sigma^2)\)

2.2 Key fixed effects (joint model)

From the joint model:

  • Intercept: \(\hat\beta_0 = -4.1875\)
  • Match played (z): \(\hat\beta_1 = 0.1083\)
  • Season (z): \(\hat\beta_2 = 0.5902\)
  • League effects (vs Bundesliga as reference):
    • La Liga: \(-0.1891\)
    • Ligue 1: \(-0.2589\)
    • Premier League: \(-0.0288\) (ns)
    • Serie A: \(-0.0511\) (ns)

All p-values for match_played_z and season_z are < 0.001.

2.3 Interpretation in plain language

  • Baseline injury rate:
    The intercept \(-4.19\) is the log injury rate at:

    • average number of matches,
    • average season (centered),
    • in the reference league (Bundesliga),
    • for an “average” team.

    Converting to a rate:

6. Practical interpretation and implications

6.1 Core findings

  • Injury rates are increasing over time (large, consistent positive season_z effect). This is possibly affected by better coverage of injuries in more recent years.
  • More matches → more injuries, with a stable ~10–15% increase per SD of match load across all leagues.
  • Baseline injury levels differ modestly between leagues, with La Liga and Ligue 1 slightly lower than Bundesliga, and Premier League/Serie A similar to Bundesliga.
  • Heterogeneity is high for intercepts and time trends, but low for the match-load effect.

6.2 What this means in football terms

  • Workload management:
    The match-load effect is robust: adding more matches reliably increases injury risk, regardless of league. This supports policies around squad rotation, fixture congestion management, and rest periods.

  • Temporal trend:
    The strong positive time trend suggests that modern football is becoming more injury-prone—possibly due to higher intensity, tighter schedules, or changes in style of play. The fact that this trend is stronger in some leagues (e.g., Premier League) hints at league-specific structural or tactical factors.

  • Cross-league comparisons:
    While leagues differ somewhat in baseline injury rates, the mechanisms linking load and time to injuries are broadly similar. This makes cross-league lessons and interventions more transferable, especially around match congestion.

7. Limitations and considerations

  • Poisson assumption:
    If there is overdispersion (variance > mean), a negative binomial model might be more appropriate. The mixed model and random effects help, but don’t fully guarantee perfect fit.
  • Standardization:
    Effects are in units of standard deviations, which is great for comparability but less intuitive for practitioners. Translating SDs into actual match counts per league would help communication.
  • Unmeasured confounders:
    No direct adjustment for training load, travel, schedule density, or rule changes—these may partly drive the time trend.
  • Exposure scaling:
    Interpretation in “per 1,000 exposure units” depends on how match_exposure is defined (minutes, hours, etc.). Making that explicit would sharpen the practical meaning.

8. Short, plain-language summary

Across the top five European leagues, teams that play more matches and seasons in more recent years have higher match injury rates. The effect of match load is consistent across leagues, while the increase over time is strong everywhere but varies in size—strongest in the Premier League, weakest in La Liga. Baseline injury levels differ somewhat between leagues, but not dramatically. Overall, the meta-analysis paints a clear picture: modern football is getting more injury-intensive, and fixture congestion is a key, stable risk factor across competitions.

## 6. Practical interpretation and implications

### 6.1 Core findings

- **Injury rates are increasing over time** (large, consistent positive `season_z` effect). This is possibly affected by better coverage of injuries in more recent years.
- **More matches → more injuries**, with a **stable ~10–15% increase per SD of match load** across all leagues.
- **Baseline injury levels differ modestly between leagues**, with La Liga and Ligue 1 slightly lower than Bundesliga, and Premier League/Serie A similar to Bundesliga.
- **Heterogeneity is high for intercepts and time trends**, but **low for the match-load effect**.

### 6.2 What this means in football terms

- **Workload management:**  
  The match-load effect is robust: **adding more matches reliably increases injury risk**, regardless of league. This supports policies around squad rotation, fixture congestion management, and rest periods.

- **Temporal trend:**  
  The strong positive time trend suggests that **modern football is becoming more injury-prone**—possibly due to higher intensity, tighter schedules, or changes in style of play. The fact that this trend is stronger in some leagues (e.g., Premier League) hints at **league-specific structural or tactical factors**.

- **Cross-league comparisons:**  
  While leagues differ somewhat in baseline injury rates, the **mechanisms linking load and time to injuries are broadly similar**. This makes cross-league lessons and interventions more transferable, especially around match congestion.

## 7. Limitations and considerations

- **Poisson assumption:**  
  If there is overdispersion (variance > mean), a negative binomial model might be more appropriate. The mixed model and random effects help, but don’t fully guarantee perfect fit.
- **Standardization:**  
  Effects are in units of **standard deviations**, which is great for comparability but less intuitive for practitioners. Translating SDs into actual match counts per league would help communication.
- **Unmeasured confounders:**  
  No direct adjustment for training load, travel, schedule density, or rule changes—these may partly drive the time trend.
- **Exposure scaling:**  
  Interpretation in “per 1,000 exposure units” depends on how `match_exposure` is defined (minutes, hours, etc.). Making that explicit would sharpen the practical meaning.

## 8. Short, plain-language summary

Across the top five European leagues, teams that **play more matches** and seasons in **more recent years** have **higher match injury rates**. The effect of **match load** is consistent across leagues, while the **increase over time** is strong everywhere but varies in size—strongest in the Premier League, weakest in La Liga. Baseline injury levels differ somewhat between leagues, but not dramatically. Overall, the meta-analysis paints a clear picture: **modern football is getting more injury-intensive, and fixture congestion is a key, stable risk factor across competitions.**
